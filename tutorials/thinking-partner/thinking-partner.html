Quick Start: The Seven Moves

One topic, seven moves, a view you can act on.

Search box vs thinking partner

Most people ask AI one question and take the one answer. A practitioner runs a short conversation that builds a calibrated, evidence-based view they could not have reached alone. These seven moves are how.

Detailed Step-by-Step Guide

Each move, why it works, with a worked example

The worked example

The video runs these moves on one real, multi-turn conversation about the future of software-engineering jobs in the AI era: a topic with genuine stakes and a lot of conflicting headlines. Watch the moves on that topic first, then run the same pattern on a question of your own.

Move 1: Ask a real question

Real stakes pull a better answer out of the model than a toy prompt does.

❌ Forgettable

"Tell me about software jobs." The model gives you its first, blandest thought.

✅ Real stakes

"Set the record straight about software jobs. There's talk of redundancies and of more work than ever. What's a genuinely useful way to think about it?"

Move 2: Drill deeper

Two pushes. First for evidence: "What does the latest research actually say?" This moves the model from opinion to data. Then for the time horizon: "What does this look like over the next five to ten years?" This forces it to project, not just describe today.

Move 3 (KEY): Ask the AI for the questions

The single biggest unlock. Instead of limiting the conversation to your own framing, ask the model to map the territory you cannot see yet.

If you could ask any questions and explore every angle of this, what would you be most curious to ask?

In the worked example the model came back with questions most people would never have asked: which roles actually shrink versus just change shape, what the data says versus the headlines, how fast the shift is in years not decades, which skills compound and which become commodities, and where judgement still beats automation.

Move 4 (KEY): Surface the hypotheses

"What working theories or hypotheses are we entertaining here?" Now you see the structure of the answer, not just the answer, and you can test each theory on its own. In the example, two surfaced clearly: demand for software tends to rise as it gets cheaper to build, and routine coding commoditises while system-level judgement does not.

Move 5 (KEY): Force calibration

"State your confidence on each claim, and name the assumptions behind it." This is the bet-weights discipline in chat form. The model splits high-confidence claims from low ones and exposes its assumptions, so you never trust an uncalibrated answer.

HIGH

Software demand grows long-term.

MEDIUM

Mid-level roles compress first.

LOW

The exact timing of the shift.

The point

You now act on the high-confidence claims and watch the low ones, instead of treating every sentence as equally true.

Move 6: Compress, then cross-validate

"Give me a single deep-research prompt that encompasses all of this." The conversation becomes a reusable tool: save it and run it again next time. Then cross-validate by running that prompt in another tool or model and feeding the result back: "Does this update your thinking? Anything unexpected?" This guards against confirmation drift.

Move 7: The retrospective

The most underrated prompt in AI, and the punchline of this lesson.

What prompt should I have used from the very start, and what would have been the most useful questions to ask?

It compresses the whole conversation into the single master question that would have unlocked it in one move. It teaches you, not just answers you: run it after any hard chat and your next opening prompt gets sharper. Save the master question; it becomes your opening move next time you face the topic.

The calibration move reuses the rubric mindset from The Evaluator's Mindset, and the hallucination awareness from AI Foundations sits underneath it. If you have done those, this move lands harder.

Troubleshooting

Common snags and fixes

One conversation, not one prompt

The power is in the multi-turn conversation, not any single prompt. Stay in one chat and keep building on the model's previous answers.

The answers stay shallow

Your opening question was probably a toy prompt. Give it real stakes and a real framing (Move 1), then drill for evidence and a time horizon (Move 2).

The "ask for questions" move returns generic questions

Give it more context first. Run Moves 1 and 2 so the model has a real topic and some evidence to work from before you ask it what it is curious about.

The model sounds equally confident about everything

That is exactly what Move 5 fixes. Explicitly ask it to mark each claim high, medium, or low confidence and to name its assumptions. Never trust an uncalibrated answer.

I cannot get a public share link

ChatGPT: use the Share button for a chatgpt.com/share/... link. If your account or workspace disables sharing, paste the conversation into a public Gist, Notion page, or Google Doc instead.

It feels slow compared to a single question

It is slower, and that is the point. A search-box answer is fast and forgettable. A thinking-partner conversation is the one you can actually act on.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Starting fresh chats for each move instead of building one conversation.
  • Skipping the three KEY moves (ask-for-questions, hypotheses, calibration). They are the whole point.
  • Trusting confident-sounding claims without forcing calibration.
  • Forgetting the retrospective. It is the move that makes you better next time.
  • Publishing private or confidential content in your shared conversation.

Video Transcript

The full walkthrough in text form

AI as a thinking partner

A few years ago it would have been hard to imagine that AI systems could become thinking partners. But here we are in 2026, and the way large language models are built makes it possible for them not only to handle language but to formulate fairly complex ideas that are genuinely useful for thinking. So let's explore how to go beyond a search box, typing one question and taking one answer, and instead build conversations that follow an evidence-based approach and help us think better.

Beyond the search box

If you say to a model "tell me about software jobs," it probably will not surface anything interesting. But if you come in with a real question, or even a voice command (I encourage you to use the microphone and talk to it naturally in ChatGPT or any other model), something like: "Let's set the record straight about software jobs. There's a lot of talk about redundancies and about more work than ever." Now you are having an actual conversation, framed in a way that is natural to you, with real stakes.

The seven moves

From there you drill deeper, pushing for evidence and for the time horizon. You ask the AI for the questions: what angles might you be missing, what are the blind spots in your own thinking? You make the model's reasoning explicit: what are your hypotheses, how did you reach this conclusion, what is the evidence? And you force calibration, pushing it to state its confidence and the assumptions behind each claim. You do not need the same prompt or format every time, and you can cross-validate the responses elsewhere, especially when the stakes are high.

The retrospective: what should I have asked?

At the end of a long, productive conversation, if you did not quite get the output you needed and then finally do, ask: "What should I have asked from the start? What prompt should I have tried at the very beginning to get here?" Try it next time you are stuck with ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini and only got there after a while. The AI itself can teach you how to talk to it better. This is meta-prompting: prompting about prompting.

Bonus: improve this prompt

A related move: say your starting prompt is a little vague, but it is the best you can come up with. Type improve this prompt and then your prompt (in quotation marks or brackets, or nothing at all). It will take your prompt and improve it, and you can run the improved version. You will be surprised how well it works.

Ask the AI for the questions

Asking the AI for the questions turns the answerer into a question-designer, and this can be an important unlock. These are the questions you did not know to ask. Say something like: "If you could ask any questions and explore every angle of this, what would you be most curious to ask?" You stop limiting the conversation to your own framing, and the model helps you map the territory and explore adjacent, relevant ideas. On the software-jobs question, for instance: which roles actually shrink and which just change shape? What does the data say versus the headlines? How fast is the shift? What skills compound and what becomes a commodity? Where does judgement still beat automation?

In my experience it stimulates my thinking. It may not give the exact answer I was looking for, but it makes me curious to learn more, to go looking for different angles and discover information myself. It is a discovery process, and it works for anything: a hobby, a sport, a personal or professional project, a career move, a trip, learning a new skill you have always wanted to learn or were afraid to ask about.

Make the reasoning visible, then calibrate

Think about it this way: if our brains somehow got a 10, 15, or 20 percent IQ boost, what would we do with it, for life in general, not just work? That is the kind of augmentation an LLM offers when you use it to think deeper and ask more meaningful questions, ones you could not easily ask other people or simply Google.

Make the reasoning visible by asking what working theories or hypotheses are being entertained, so you can see the underlying frameworks behind an answer. Then ask the model to state its confidence on each claim and name the assumptions behind it. In my own setup I created something called bet-weights. A neural network is made of weights: the strengths of the connections, the parallel to the connections between neurons in our brains that let us learn. When you ask a model to bet its weights on an answer, you are asking it to wager everything it has that the output is correct. I find it works: sometimes it replies that it would not actually bet its weights on the previous answer. It is a bit playful, but it gives you transparency on how confident it really is, rather than a uniformly confident tone.

Play with it

So today, try it. Take a real question you are weighing, a decision you are trying to make, or something you are genuinely curious about, and have a chat. Not for work: try it on something you actually care about, where there is some enthusiasm. I always encourage you to tinker with this technology. Use it for fun, to create fun things with and for your children and family. Keep asking those hard questions; it makes you better at talking to these systems, and the skill transfers nicely to work. Keep these moves in mind to improve your AI literacy and proficiency. I hope this helps. Thank you very much. Well done.
