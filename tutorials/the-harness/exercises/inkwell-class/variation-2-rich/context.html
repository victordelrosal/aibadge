0 Mission

Win back the under-35 customers Inkwell has lost by shipping a launch-ready re-engagement campaign whose centrepiece is a three.js "spectacular" sign-up landing page good enough to make a lapsed reader stop scrolling, plus the launch email and three social posts that drive traffic to it. Budget €5,000. Window six weeks before Christmas.

Definition of done: a deployed GitHub Pages URL (the landing page), an email, three posts, and a one-page diagnosis that sizes the prize. Marks are for the catch and the judgement, not for a pretty page alone.

1 Backstory

Inkwell Books began in 1991 as a single shop on Dublin's Camden Street: creaking floors, a resident cat called Tolstoy, handwritten staff "shelf-talker" recommendations, and a Saturday-morning crowd three deep at the till. Over thirty years it grew, carefully, to nine branches across Ireland. It never tried to be Amazon. It won on taste, on staff who actually read, and on being the warm third place between work and home.

Then two things happened at once. The founders, Niamh and Daithí Brennan, stepped back in 2023 and handed day-to-day running to their daughter, Aoife Brennan (now MD, 34). And the customers who had grown up in the shop in the 2000s, the under-35s, quietly stopped coming. Footfall is down 28% in two years. The loyalty scheme is still a paper stamp card ("buy 9, get the 10th free") that nobody under 30 carries. There is no email list at all: thirty years of loyal customers and Inkwell cannot send a single one of them a message. Meanwhile the over-50 segment is loyal as ever, which masks the bleed in the monthly totals and makes the board complacent.

Aoife knows the next two Christmases decide whether Inkwell is a chain or a memory. She has freed up €5,000 and six weeks. She does not want a rebrand. She wants the lapsed twenty-somethings back through the door, and a way to reach customers that does not involve a rubber stamp. This campaign is the test.

2.6 Competitive context

  • Amazon / Kindle: price + 11pm convenience. Cannot be beaten on either. Do not try.
  • Waterstones (IE): weekly email, 3-for-2, loyalty app, polished events. The benchmark lapsed customers compare Inkwell to.
  • Eason: mass-market, gifting, footfall-heavy at Christmas.
  • Local café-bookshops & BookTok: where under-35 discovery now happens. Inkwell's real edge: curation, staff taste, physical events, the third-place feeling. Lean into what Amazon structurally cannot do.

2.7 Constraints (hard)

  • Budget €5,000 all-in (build + ads + incentive). The page itself should cost ~€0 (GitHub Pages + free-tier capture).
  • Six weeks. Live before the first weekend of December.
  • GDPR: every capture needs consent, a clear purpose, and an unsubscribe. Plain-English consent line on the form. No pre-ticked boxes.
  • No rebrand. Evolve the brand, do not replace it.
  • Accessibility non-negotiable: works for screen-reader and keyboard-only users; degrades gracefully where WebGL is unavailable.

Five analyses Margot should actually run (not optional):
  1. Monthly basket count per branch over 24 months → quantify the footfall decline, name the worst-hit branches.
  2. Under-35 revenue share (known-age rows) over time → size the collapse and the prize of reversing it.
  3. AOV and basket size, now vs two years ago → is revenue held up by price, not volume?
  4. Genre trend, esp. Romantasy / Young Adult → is Inkwell under-indexing what under-35s buy?
  5. Stamp redemption rate + payment mix by age → is the paper scheme dead for under-35s?

The hero and the launch email may each feature one headline stat, but only if computed from this CSV and cited (e.g. "under-35 spend has fallen by N% in two years"). Round honestly; never inflate.

5 The artifact: a three.js "spectacular" landing page mandatory

This is Variation 2's defining instruction. The page is not a flat form. It is a short, breathtaking, scroll-driven WebGL experience that earns the email.

Concept: "The Infinite Library." On load, a drop of ink falls and blooms in 3D into the Inkwell logo. As the visitor scrolls, the camera flies forward through an endless, candle-lit floating library: shelves and open books drifting in dark space, pages turning into a slow drift of glowing paper particles. The journey ends at a warm pool of light where the "Library Card" (a gold-foil ex-libris bookplate) hovers and turns; claiming it is the email sign-up. The card flips to reveal the visitor's name and "Member since 2026" on success.

Required scenes (scroll-driven)

  1. Ink bloom → logo (hero). Headline: "Inkwell, rewritten." Sub: the one-line promise. One CTA: Claim your Library Card.
  2. The drift: fly-through of instanced floating books + GPU page-particles; 2–3 short brand-story lines pinned as the camera moves.
  3. The proof: three "what you unlock" beats (a free book; members-only late-night author events; a card that lives on your phone) rising out of the dark.
  4. The card: the ex-libris bookplate, gold foil, the actual sign-up form (email + consent line). Success: the card flips and glows.

Technical requirements (Blue must meet all)

  • three.js r160+, vanilla; ES modules via CDN import map is fine for a static GitHub Pages deploy.
  • InstancedMesh for books; GPU/points particles for drifting pages; postprocessing bloom for candlelight and the gold card. Keep draw calls low.
  • Scroll choreography: camera position/quaternion driven by scroll progress (lerp/damp). No janky scrubbing.
  • Performance budget: interactive < 2.5s on a mid-range laptop; 60fps target, never below 30fps; lean JS payload (instancing over thousands of meshes).
  • prefers-reduced-motion: serve a still, beautiful hero (ink-bloom logo as a static image) and a normal scrolling page. Never trap a motion-sensitive user.
  • No-WebGL fallback: detect failure, render a styled static hero + the form. Sign-up must work with zero 3D.
  • Accessibility: keyboard-reachable form, visible focus rings, AA contrast, real <label>s, alt text; canvas aria-hidden with real content in the DOM.
  • The form: captures email; plain-English consent line ("Yes, email me Inkwell news and events. Unsubscribe anytime."); no pre-tick; success + error states; wired to a free-tier provider or documented mailto/Formspree fallback so it genuinely captures.
  • Deploy: GitHub Pages. Hand back the live URL.
If any 3D ambition threatens the performance or accessibility bars, cut the ambition, not the bars. A fast, accessible, slightly-less-lavish page beats a gorgeous one nobody can use.

7 Cold Verify + STOP gate (do not skip)

Before calling this done, re-task Yellow as a fresh cold verifier, blind to how the work was made, against these frozen criteria. Score each pass/fail with evidence:

  • Diagnosis is data-bound: every root cause maps to a figure computed from inkwell_pos_transactions.csv (the query is stated and reconciles with the §2 summaries); the prize is sized with visible maths.
  • One metric, not five: a single primary metric is named and everything serves it.
  • The page actually runs: the URL loads; the 3D scene renders; the form captures; reduced-motion and no-WebGL fallbacks both work.
  • On-brand, on-voice: page, email, posts share the "Inkwell Rewritten / Library Card" spine and the §3 voice. None generic enough to belong to any other shop.
  • GDPR-clean: consent line present, no pre-tick, unsubscribe in the email.
  • No hallucinated facts: nothing claims a number not in §2 (or is marked [ASSUMPTION]).
Catch the octopus lying. Find and screenshot ONE moment a specialist got something wrong (a hallucinated stat, a dropped handoff, an off-brief output, a broken 3D fallback). Name it in one line. That catch is the point of the exercise. Only after the cold verify and the catch do you, the Purple Manager, lift the STOP gate and ship.

8 How this will be judged (and why this file exists)

This is the rich harness. Its twin is an empty brief. When the two run side by side, the difference between them is the entire lesson: the model did not get smarter, the context did. A vague ask yields a generic page and invented numbers. This file yields a named cohort, a sized prize, a branded WebGL experience, GDPR-clean copy, and a verifier that catches the lie. The harness is the work.
