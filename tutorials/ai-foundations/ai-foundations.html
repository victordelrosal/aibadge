Detailed Step-by-Step Guide

Each of the six ideas, in plain English

1. Pattern matching, not thinking

At its core a model predicts the next word. Give it The cat sat on the ___ and, from everything it was trained on (the internet, books, billions or trillions of words), it scores every possible next token and picks from the top:

mat 62%floor 21%roof 9%keyboard 1%

Then it does it again, one token at a time. Three consequences follow:

  • It is fluent, not factual. Fluency comes from patterns in text; truth has to be checked separately.
  • It has no memory of you. Each turn it only sees what is in front of it. Nothing is recalled by default.
  • It is confident either way. A right answer and a wrong one are produced the same way, so confidence is not a signal of correctness.

2. Training data and the cutoff

A model learned the world up to a training cutoff date. Inside that data: how language, code, and concepts work, public facts up to the cutoff, and patterns across billions of documents. After the cutoff: last week's news, this morning's price, your private files, anything that happened since. Ask a model what day it is and it often guesses. The fix is to give it the context, or use a tool that can look things up (internet access complements the training data but is not part of the main "brain").

3. Tokens

AI reads text in chunks called tokens, not whole words. A token is roughly four characters; about 750 words is about 1,000 tokens. "Tokenisation" splits into several tokens, while common words are one each, and spaces and punctuation count too. Why it matters:

  • Cost: most tools bill per token, in and out, so longer prompts cost more.
  • Length: every model has a token budget; very long inputs get truncated.
  • Languages: some languages use more tokens per word, so they cost more.

4. Context window

The model only sees what fits in its context window: your prompt, your files, and every reply share that one window. Past the limit, the earliest text falls out of view, as if it never existed.

  • Long chats forget the start. If a conversation runs long, restate the key facts near the end. (Newer tools re-inject or summarise the start to soften this, but it still pays to remember.)
  • Big files can overflow. A model with a small window cannot reason over a whole long document at once.
  • Put what matters last. Recent context carries the most weight, so lead with the ask, not the backstory.

5. Temperature

Temperature is one dial: predictable or surprising. At temperature 0 the model sticks to the most likely word (the 62% one); raise it and the model is freer to pick the second or third option, so it gets more creative.

Temperature 0 · predictable

"Give me a name for a coffee shop." → The Daily Grind, The Daily Grind, The Daily Grind. Repeatable and on-task. Use for facts, code, extraction.

Temperature 1.2 · creative

"Give me a name for a coffee shop." → Cloud & Cardamom, The Velvet Bean, Loiter House Coffee. Varied but less reliable. Use for brainstorms and first drafts.

Most chat apps hide this dial; it is mainly a developer setting in APIs. But knowing it exists explains why the same prompt can answer differently each time, and in a normal chat you can simply ask the model to "be more creative" to a similar effect.

6. Hallucinations

Made-up answers are a feature, not a glitch. A model that always predicts the next likely word will sometimes predict a confident, plausible falsehood. Ask for the title of a paper that does not exist and it may invent a specific, confident citation, complete with journal and volume. Three signatures to watch for:

Too specific

Exact figures, names, dates, or citations that would be hard to actually know.

Too confident

No hedging at all on something genuinely uncertain or obscure.

Unverifiable

You cannot find an independent source that confirms it.

The fix is your own fact-checking: confirm the link, the paper, the book, the source actually exists, especially for high-stakes work. The model generates answers from its neural network; it is not Google looking information up.

Putting it together

These six ideas explain your everyday choices: you phrase prompts tightly because of tokens, you upload the file because of the context window, and you check the output because of hallucination risk. Mechanics first, then craft.

Common Questions

Quick answers to the usual confusions

The one-line takeaway

An LLM predicts the next token from patterns it has seen. Everything surprising it does follows from that one fact.

If it is just predicting words, how is it so good?

Because the patterns in trillions of words encode a huge amount of structure about language, code, and concepts. Fluency and usefulness are real; factual accuracy is the part you still have to check.

Why does it sound so confident when it is wrong?

Confidence is not a signal. A correct answer and a made-up one are produced by the same next-word process, so tone tells you nothing about truth.

It does not know today's date or recent news

That is the training cutoff. Either give it the information directly, or use a tool with internet access so it can look things up.

It forgot something I said earlier in a long chat

Context window. The earliest text fell out of view. Restate the key facts near the end, or start a fresh chat with just what matters.

Same prompt, different answers every time

Temperature. Higher temperature lets it pick less-likely words. For repeatable results, ask for precise output; for ideas, let it roam.

It invented a citation that does not exist

A hallucination. Watch for the three signatures (too specific, too confident, unverifiable) and verify any fact that matters against an independent source.

Key terms

  • Token: a chunk of text (~4 characters) the model reads and bills by.
  • Context window: the total text the model can see at once.
  • Training cutoff: the date after which the model knows nothing on its own.
  • Temperature: the randomness dial, predictable (low) to creative (high).
  • Hallucination: a confident, plausible, but false output.

Video Transcript

The full walkthrough in text form

What is under the hood

Over the last few weeks we have been using AI and pushing it to its limits, but we have not really discussed what is under the hood, what makes AI work. So let's talk about some of the foundations: why large language models work and how. No maths, no jargon, just the principles. If you have ever wondered what makes AI so confident about its outputs, the key is to understand pattern matching, how it is trained, and the ideas of tokens, context windows, temperature, and hallucinations (also called confabulations).

Pattern matching

In short, the model predicts the next word. When you ask it to complete a sentence, such as "the cat sat on the," it looks at all its training. Remember it has ingested the internet, books, everything companies could gather for training: billions, perhaps trillions, of words and the way they are constructed. So with a high degree of confidence it can say the next word is most likely, say, 62% probable, with other words at lower percentages. It scores every possible next token, picks from the top, and does that over and over. It has no specific memory of you, so you have to remind it of the context. Either way it is confident in what it produced, because that confidence comes from the statistics of the patterns it has seen.

Training data and the cutoff

It has learned the world up to a certain date. There is a training cutoff: whatever is before it is inside the training data, whatever is after is not. So for recent news or any recent knowledge, it simply is not there. It will not know what day it is unless you tell it. This is solved by giving the model internet access, so in addition to its training data it can fetch information in real time, but that is a complement; it is not part of the main brain.

Tokens

AI reads text in chunks, not whole words, and these are called tokens. A token is roughly four characters; about 750 words is about 1,000 tokens. Tokenisation is surprisingly important. Common words are one token; spaces and punctuation count too. Most tools bill per token in and out, so longer prompts cost more, and length impacts the token budget: very long input gets truncated if it exceeds what the model can ingest. Some languages use more tokens per word, so they can cost more. These are practical considerations.

Context window

The model only sees what fits. Your prompt, your files, and every reply share one window. Beyond that limit, whatever is out of scope is not seen. So in a very long conversation, if your model has a small context window, what you said at the beginning is forgotten; it does not exist for the model. The newest message is seen, but not the very start. Companies have softened this by re-injecting or summarising the beginning, but it is good to keep in mind that there is a context window and a long chat can lose its start. Big files can overflow too, so it is a good idea to put what matters most at the end, where the recent context carries the most weight.

Temperature

The other important idea is temperature, which has to do with creativity. A low temperature means it sticks to the most likely word, the 62% one. If you increase the temperature, the model can also choose the next most likely options, so it gets more creative. This is more of a developer setting you tweak in APIs; it is not something you necessarily do chatting with AI directly, but you can tell it to be more creative and in theory that works as well.

Hallucinations

The other issue, which we still see though not as much as at the beginning, is hallucinations: made-up answers that are problematic, also called confabulations. A model predicting the next likely word will sometimes make up a confident yet totally false answer. It might give you the title of a paper that does not exist. So it is always a good idea to keep an eye out and do your own fact-checking: make sure the link exists, the paper exists, the book or source exists. If something looks too specific or too confident but is unverifiable, do your own homework, especially for highly sensitive projects.

What to do next

Now you understand the mechanics, so you can spot hallucinations and remember this is a probabilistic machine, not Google looking information up: it generates answers from its neural network. Notice tokens, and remember context windows, so answers at the very start of a long chat can be forgotten. Try the idea of temperature: see if you can ask for more creative answers as opposed to strictly probabilistic ones. Carry this as an explorer, and keep looking at more technical explanations and diving deeper. Ironically, the LLM itself can explain how it works, so by all means follow your curiosity in the process of your own exploration. Thanks for your time, and see you in the next video.
