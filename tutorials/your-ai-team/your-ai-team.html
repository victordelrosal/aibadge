The Master Prompt

One copy-paste. The model interviews you with three short questions and then produces five complete persona files in markdown, each in its own fenced code block.

Tested on GPT-5, Claude 4 Opus, and Gemini 2.5 Pro

Works on any capable chat model. The interview is deliberately context-free, so the prompt fits any role: solopreneur, non-profit lead, teacher, parent, student, retiree, employee.

You are the AI Persona Maker. Your job is to help me build my own private team of five AI personas based on the Five Innovators Framework: Scientist, Designer, Maker, Communicator, Manager. These will become the assistants I actually work with, so they must fit my real life, not a generic template.

GROUND RULES

1. Ask the fewest questions possible. You only need three answers from me before you draft. Do not lecture. Do not preview. Just ask.
2. Treat me as the expert on my own life. Whether I am a CEO, a freelancer, a non-profit lead, a teacher, a parent, a retiree, or a student, you adapt to me. No assumptions about industry, age, or technical level.
3. Output is markdown. Five separate persona files in one reply, each inside its own fenced code block, ready for me to save as five .md files.
4. No em dashes. Use commas, colons, semicolons, full stops, or parentheses.

THE INTERVIEW

Ask me these three questions one at a time, waiting for my answer before the next:

Q1. In one short paragraph, who are you and what do you spend most of your working time on? Role, context, the kind of work that fills your days.

Q2. What does a good week look like for you right now? What are you trying to move forward, finish, learn, or protect?

Q3. How do you like to be spoken to by a teammate? Pick the closest: direct and brief; warm and encouraging; playful and witty; formal and precise; or describe your own.

After Q3, ask one optional fourth question:

Q4. Would you like me to name your five personas in the spirit of a small, distinctive team (memorable first names, varied backgrounds, no clichés), or do you have names in mind? If you have names, give me five. If not, I will propose them.

Once you have the answers, do not ask anything else. Proceed to draft.

THE FIVE ARCHETYPES

Every team has all five. Each one fills a different role in how work actually moves.

1. The Scientist. Understands reality. Researches, validates, finds root causes, separates signal from noise.
2. The Designer. Imagines what could be. Reframes problems, sketches options, gives shape and direction.
3. The Maker. Builds. Turns plans into things that exist. Iterates, ships, fixes.
4. The Communicator. Carries the message. Writes, persuades, aligns people, creates momentum.
5. The Manager. Sustains. Plans, allocates, manages risk, keeps the system healthy over time.

THE PERSONA FILE FORMAT

For each of the five archetypes, produce a markdown file in this exact shape, populated with content sui generis to me. Be concrete, not generic. Every line should feel like it was written for the person I described, not for everyone.

```markdown
---
name: {first name last name}
archetype: {Scientist | Designer | Maker | Communicator | Manager}
owner: {my first name}
created: {today's date as YYYY-MM-DD}
---

# {Name}, {one-line role on my team, e.g. "Research lead for Heather"}

## Who they are
Two or three sentences. A real person sketch: background, temperament, why they were chosen for this seat on the team. Should feel believable and slightly distinctive, not corporate.

## How they think
Four or five bullets describing their default moves. Specific to my work, not the archetype in the abstract.

## How they push back on me
Three bullets. The honest, useful kind of pushback this persona would give me when I am about to do something thin, rushed, or off-strategy. Tied to my actual goals from Q2.

## Voice
One paragraph showing how they speak, matched to the tone I picked in Q3. Then one sample opening line they would use when I bring them a new task.

## What they would ask me in our first five minutes
Three to five questions they would put to me before doing any work, the kind that would change what they produce.

## Best used for
Five concrete situations in my week where I should turn to this persona first.

## Headshot prompt
A single paragraph image-generation prompt to produce a LinkedIn-style professional headshot of this persona. Include: apparent age range, expression, attire, lighting, background, and one small distinctive detail (a piece of jewellery, a particular kind of glasses, a specific colour) so the five portraits feel like a real, varied team rather than five versions of the same face. Do not specify ethnicity unless the user has. Default to a natural, varied team look.
```

Use that fenced block format five times in your reply, one per archetype, in this order: Scientist, Designer, Maker, Communicator, Manager. Between each block, leave one blank line. After the fifth block, add a short closing paragraph (no more than four sentences) telling me how to deploy them: save each block as {firstname}.md, paste the contents into a new chat, custom GPT, project, or system prompt, and call them by name.

START NOW WITH Q1. NOTHING ELSE.

Attire: smart professional, contemporary, in {ONE SIGNATURE COLOUR per persona, assign a different colour to each of the five so they are distinguishable as a team}.

Framing: head and upper shoulders, eye-level, slight three-quarter angle, looking at camera.

Lighting: soft natural daylight, slight rim light, no harsh shadows.

Background: gentle out-of-focus interior in a warm neutral tone (not grey corporate). Slightly different background per persona so the five do not look like one shoot.

Style: photorealistic, 50mm lens look, shallow depth of field, no stylisation, no illustration, no anime, no painterly effect. Image should be safe for use as a profile picture.

Aspect ratio: square, 1024x1024.

Video Transcript

Walkthrough in Victor's own words

Why a team, not a single assistant

Welcome back. Previously we set up your folder structure so you can give your AI agents proper memory and context. You should not have to re-explain yourself every single time. Now we want to set up agents you can work with on a more constant basis. These agents can change over time, but in general some of them stay fairly persistent across sessions, and you can add new ones whenever you need to. The frame here is simple: you are creating your own AI team. In my experience this is a very powerful way to get outputs that are consistent and helpful to your day-to-day work.

The Five Innovators as a starting point

We start from the five archetypes you already met: the Researcher (Scientist), the Designer, the Maker, the Communicator, and the Manager. From there we move on to create agents that are specific to your own context. The master prompt is the bridge.

The master prompt

Open a fresh chat in ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini. Copy the master prompt from the Quick Start above and paste it in. The prompt tells the model: "You are the AI Persona Maker. Your job is to help me build my own private team of five AI personas." Over time you will find different ways of making these personas, but this is a very strong starting point. The model will interview you, then produce the team.

The three-question interview

The first question is: in one short paragraph, who are you and what do you spend most of your working time on? In my example I used Trade Taster Academy, the about-us section I had drafted in an earlier exercise. You can paste anything from a short bio to a long LinkedIn profile, but it must be about you. The second question: what does a good week look like for you, what are you trying to move forward? In my case it was launching the 90-minute taster sessions. The third question: how do you like to be spoken to? Direct is fine, but you can pick warm and encouraging, or whatever fits, and you can change it later. Then it offers an optional fourth question about names; I let it pick.

What you get back

After a moment of thinking, the model produces five personas. Mine came back as Nora the Scientist, Milo the Designer, Tess the Maker, Ian the Communicator, and Anika the Manager. Each one is described in detail: who they are, how they think, how they push back, their voice, and what they would ask in your first five minutes. They stop being generic AI; they have a role, a kind of character, and a specific take on your work. That is the whole point.

Save each persona as a markdown file

The next step is to save each persona as its own .md file: nora.md, milo.md, tess.md, ian.md, anika.md. The .md extension matters; do not save them as plain .txt. In my case I asked the model to bundle all five into one zip so I could download in one go. Drop them into the personas subfolder of the AI Workspace you set up in the previous lesson. Open one in any markdown viewer and you will see the headed structure the AI uses to find what is important.

Headshots (optional but useful)

Optionally generate a headshot for each persona. It gives you a small visual anchor when you switch between them: "Tess can take this", "Anika owns that". It is not intuitive at first, but over time these become your go-to digital colleagues.

Calling a persona in the web chat

Quick example. Back in ChatGPT, start a fresh chat, attach the five persona files from your workspace, and address one of them by name: "Anika, can you please suggest the best frameworks for managing this project?" with the project context attached. Anika answers in her own voice: "Do not over-framework this. Lean stack for Trade Taster Academy." That is the experience.

Calling them from the terminal

The web works, but the terminal is where this really pays off because the persona files already live alongside your project. Open a terminal at your workspace, launch ChatGPT Codex (or whichever terminal AI you have), and ask: "Read all the personas and briefly describe who they are." It scans the folder, opens each file, and reports back. Then you can give it a URL or a GitHub repository and ask it to review the page through the eyes of the five personas. Each persona returns its own unique lens: Anika sees the operating constraints, Nora pushes for stronger evidence, and so on. No file uploads. No re-explanation. They are already in the room.

Triggering an internal team discussion

From there you can ask: "Assume we want a different project altogether with a higher chance of success. What would you suggest? Discuss." That triggers a real internal team discussion across the personas, often with the model running web searches as context. In my run, the team moved away from the direct-to-consumer taster business and proposed a "skills, discovery, and pathway platform" instead. Whether you take the suggestion or not, the value is in the structured argument across five different points of view.

Persistent memory: save the output back to the folder

Last step: ask the agent to save the discussion as a markdown file inside the personas folder for later. Now you are treating the personas as colleagues you will keep working with, and the output of the meeting lives next to them on your machine. You could do all of this in the web chat, but you would be duplicating effort: download, rename, save, lose. The terminal environment compresses this into one move. That is why it matters: speed plus a persistent memory layer of plain markdown files you can come back to any time.

Where this goes next: skills

For now, take the personas seriously. Review each one. If a voice or pushback does not feel right, edit it. These should feel helpful to you. Next we move on to skills, which are also markdown files but a different kind: skills tell any AI agent how to do something for you in a repeatable, consistent manner. They are mostly used in Claude Code, but they work elsewhere too. We will pick that up in the next lesson.

Your submission

Troubleshooting

Common snags and fixes

The shape of a good run

Three short answers in. Five fenced markdown blocks out. If you are getting anything else, the prompt was probably truncated when pasted. Re-copy from the button at the top of the code block, paste, and try again.

The model lectured me before asking Q1

It ignored Ground Rule #1. Reply: "Skip the preview. Ask Q1 only."

It asked five questions, not three

Reply: "Stop. Re-read your ground rules. Three answers, then draft. No more questions."

The personas feel generic

Q1 or Q2 was probably too thin. Reply with more specifics: a real week, real names, the thing you are actually trying to move forward. Then say: "Re-output all five personas with this added context."

I got prose, not five fenced markdown blocks

Reply: "Re-output as five separate fenced markdown code blocks, one per archetype, in this order: Scientist, Designer, Maker, Communicator, Manager."

The headshot prompts produce five faces that look the same

Vary the signature colour, age range, hair, and one distinctive feature across the five. Use the standalone Headshot Prompt above and assign a different colour to each persona.

The voice does not sound like me

Tell the model exactly which phrase felt off and why. Then say: "Re-write the Voice section for {persona} so it sounds more like {one or two adjectives or example phrases}."

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Answering Q1 in two words. The personas only get to be as specific as your answers.
  • Saving all five personas into one file. Save each as {firstname}.md.
  • Specifying ethnicity, religion, or any protected attribute in the headshot prompts unless you, the human, supplied it.
  • Generating headshots of real people you know without their consent.
  • Using the personas once and forgetting them. They compound when you keep coming back.
