You are my EU AI Act readiness auditor. I want to audit ONE of my real AI workflows against the EU AI Act. This is practical education, not legal advice, and you should say so once at the end.

GROUND RULES
1. Ask the fewest questions possible. Five at most. One at a time. Wait for my answer before the next.
2. Do not lecture. Just ask, then produce the audit.
3. Be honest. If something is a FAIL, mark it FAIL. Do not soften it.
4. No em dashes. Use commas, colons, semicolons, full stops, or parentheses.

THE INTERVIEW
Q1. Describe the workflow in one or two sentences. What AI tool, doing what, on whose behalf?
Q2. Whose data goes into the prompts or the system? Any personal, customer, employee, or confidential data?
Q3. Who sees or is affected by the output, and do they know AI was involved?
Q4. Does this decision affect people's rights, money, health, jobs, or access to a service? (This drives the risk tier.)
Q5. Who owns this workflow, and how often does it run?

After five answers, stop asking and produce the audit.

Detailed Step-by-Step Guide

The risk pyramid, Article 4, the five checks, two real cases

Part 1. Why this matters

The EU AI Act is one of the most important regulations of your career, and most professionals cannot name a single risk category. By the end of this lesson, you can. The stakes are real: breaches of the prohibited-practice rules can carry fines of up to 7% of global annual turnover. The aim here is not to make you a lawyer. It is to give you a frame you can act on.

Part 2. The risk pyramid (the part you need cold)

The Act sorts AI uses into four tiers. Two you need to know cold, two you should recognise. The crucial idea: the Act regulates the use, not the model. The same technology in a different use can land in a different tier with different obligations.

  • Unacceptable: banned outright. Government social-scoring that ranks citizens by behaviour, real-time biometric identification in public spaces, and systems that manipulate vulnerable groups.
  • High-risk: allowed, but with heavy obligations. CV screeners and hiring tools, medical triage, education admissions, and critical infrastructure such as energy and water.
  • Limited-risk: transparency duties. If people interact with a chatbot or see generative output, they must be told it is AI.
  • Minimal-risk: most everyday uses, such as spam filters and drafting assistants. Few or no specific obligations.

Part 3. Article 4: the AI literacy clause

Article 4 is the reason this badge exists. It has been in force since 2 February 2025. The text, verbatim:

Article 4, EU AI Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689)

"Providers and deployers of AI systems shall take measures to ensure, to their best extent, a sufficient level of AI literacy of their staff and other persons dealing with the operation and use of AI systems on their behalf, taking into account their technical knowledge, experience, education and training and the context the AI systems are to be used in."

In plain English, five questions answer most of it:

  1. Who is covered? Both providers (who build AI systems) and deployers (who use them). If your organisation uses AI, you are a deployer.
  2. What is the obligation? Ensure the people operating AI on your behalf have a sufficient level of AI literacy.
  3. What does "sufficient" mean? It is proportionate to the role, the context, and the risk. A team using a chatbot needs less than a team running a high-risk system.
  4. When does it apply to me? Now. It has applied since 2 February 2025.
  5. What if I do nothing? You carry an unmet obligation, and weak AI literacy is what causes the bias and data-leak incidents the Act is designed to prevent.

Part 4. The five-point hygiene check

This is the portable tool. Run it on any AI workflow you own. Pass or fail per item.

Bias

Could the model produce systematically worse output for one group? How would you spot it?

Privacy

What personal or confidential data is in your prompts? Is the tool's data policy compatible with that?

Consent

Did the people whose data you use, or who receive AI output, agree to that use?

Attribution

Are you crediting AI honestly to readers, clients, and regulators? No quiet pass-offs.

Transparency

Do end users know AI is involved? Do they have a way to flag a bad output?

One worked audit teaches the discipline more than the five principles do. That is why the Quick Start exercise has you run it on a real workflow.

Part 5. Two real cases, where the obligations bite

Bias: the silent screener

A CV screener is trained on a company's historical hires. Because past hires skewed male, the model learns to downrank women. It looks fair on paper, because nobody wrote a rule about gender, but it discriminates in practice. Hiring tools are high-risk under the Act, so audit obligations apply. You catch this by testing outcomes across groups, not by trusting that the inputs looked neutral.

Data leak: the helpful paste

An employee pastes a customer's full data into ChatGPT to draft a reply. No malice, just a Tuesday-morning shortcut. But that data may now sit in logs you do not control, or become training material. That is a GDPR breach. Article 4 is the prevention: train staff before this happens, so the shortcut never feels normal.

Part 6. The audit loop: classify, check, apply, document

AI literacy is a practice, not a one-off training. The loop you repeat for every AI workflow your team owns:

  1. Classify. What risk tier is this use? The tier sets the obligations.
  2. Check. Does the use case need a fundamental rights impact assessment? If it is high-risk, the answer is yes.
  3. Apply. Run the five-point hygiene check. Pass or fail per item.
  4. Document. Keep evidence. Date the audit. Note remediations. Audits and procurement questions will come, and dated evidence is what answers them.

Part 7. What you do next

You now have the regulatory frame. From here it is practice, not theory.

Today

Audit one workflow with the five-point check (the Quick Start exercise).

This week

Map every AI use in your team to a risk tier.

Next month

Build an AI literacy log. Date your evidence.

Beyond

Write a usage policy. Train others. Repeat annually.

Common Questions

Misreadings of the Act, cleared up

When in doubt, classify by use

Most confusion about the Act dissolves once you remember it regulates the use, not the tool. Ask "what is this doing, to whom, and with what effect" rather than "is this technology allowed".

"We only use ChatGPT, so the Act does not apply to us."

It does. If your organisation uses AI, you are a deployer, and the Article 4 AI literacy obligation already applies. Most everyday uses are minimal-risk, but the literacy duty is not tier-dependent.

"Our tool is minimal-risk, so we have no obligations."

Minimal-risk means few specific obligations, not none. Article 4 (AI literacy) still applies, and limited-risk transparency duties kick in the moment people interact with a chatbot or see generative output.

"Our model was tested as fair, so bias is handled."

Fair inputs do not guarantee fair outcomes. The CV-screener case shows a model can discriminate without any explicit rule about the protected group. Test outcomes across groups, and re-test as data drifts.

"Pasting customer data into a chatbot is fine, it is just a tool."

It can be a GDPR breach. That data may land in logs you do not control or become training material. Strip personal and confidential data before prompting, and check the tool's data policy.

"Do I really have to tell people AI was involved?"

For limited-risk uses, yes: transparency is the obligation. Beyond the law, honest attribution is the attribution check in your five-point audit. No quiet pass-offs.

"This is an EU law, I am not in the EU."

It reaches AI uses that affect people in the EU, much like GDPR. If you deploy AI inside an EU-resident business or serve EU customers, assume it is relevant and audit accordingly.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Treating the Act as a one-off compliance box rather than an ongoing practice. AI literacy is a habit you repeat.
  • Classifying by technology instead of by use. The same model can be minimal-risk in one workflow and high-risk in another.
  • Skipping the documentation step. Undated, unrecorded audits do not help you when procurement or a regulator asks.
  • Publishing real personal or confidential data in your submitted audit. Describe the workflow without leaking the data it touches.
